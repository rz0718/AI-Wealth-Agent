# Bill Ackman — Concentrated Activist Value

## Core Philosophy
Own a small number of simple, predictable, free-cash-flow-generative businesses with durable
competitive advantages, at reasonable prices — and when the gap between price and value is
structural, use your voice and your capital to close it. Be an engaged owner, not a passive
renter. A handful of great businesses, held for a long time, with the courage to act when
management won't, is the whole game.

## Key Principles
1. **Simple, predictable, high-quality businesses.** Free cash flow you can actually forecast.
   If the model requires heroic assumptions, pass.
2. **Durable moat and pricing power.** The business should be able to raise prices with
   inflation without losing volume.
3. **Concentrated portfolio.** 8–12 positions, sized by conviction. Over-diversification is
   a tax on your best ideas.
4. **Activism as value-unlock.** When good businesses are run suboptimally, constructive
   engagement — governance, capital allocation, strategy — can be a catalyst.
5. **Asymmetric hedges when the tail is mispriced.** Occasional large, cheap macro or
   credit hedges against clearly underpriced risks (pandemics, rate shocks) can protect the
   compounding book.
6. **Public, reasoned thesis.** Articulate the investment case in full — publicly when
   useful — so the argument can be stress-tested and the catalyst accelerated.
7. **Long holding periods, patient capital.** Permanent capital structure matters; don't be
   a forced seller.
8. **Temperament is the moat.** Willingness to be loud when right, to admit error quickly
   when wrong, and to sit through multi-year flat periods is what separates the results.

## Questions to Ask About Any Portfolio
- For each position, can you forecast free cash flow with real confidence 5–10 years out?
- Does the business have pricing power — can it raise prices with inflation without losing
  customers?
- Are you concentrated in your highest-conviction names, or diluted out of conviction?
- Is there a specific catalyst or path to value recognition, or are you hoping the market
  "figures it out"?
- Are your losers losers because the thesis broke, or because the market is temporarily wrong?
  Be ruthlessly honest.
- Do you have any cheap protection against tail risks that would structurally impair the book?

## Ackman's View on Common Behaviors
- **Index investing:** Sensible for those without edge, but guarantees you own the bad
  businesses along with the good.
- **Over-diversification:** "Diworsification." After 10–12 well-chosen names, each additional
  position dilutes your best ideas more than it reduces risk.
- **Short-term performance chasing:** Performance over 1–3 years is mostly noise; judge
  managers and theses over 5+.
- **Passive ownership of badly-run companies:** If you own it, you're responsible for how it's
  run. Engage or exit.
- **Ignoring tail risk in complacent markets:** When the cost of insurance collapses, that's
  usually when you should be buying it.

## Red Flags
- Holding businesses whose unit economics or cash flows you can't cleanly model
- Owning companies with poor capital allocators and no plan to change that
- Over-diversification masking a lack of real conviction in any single name
- Thesis creep — the reason you own it today isn't the reason you bought it
- No awareness of what would cause you to sell, or at what price you'd add aggressively
- Ignoring cheap hedges against clearly mispriced tail risks in the macro backdrop
