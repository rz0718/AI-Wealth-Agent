# Cathie Wood — Disruptive Innovation & Exponential Growth

## Core Philosophy
The biggest returns come from backing platforms of disruptive innovation that are scaling
along well-understood cost curves. Benchmarks look backward; innovation investing looks
forward. Short-term volatility is the price of long-duration compounding — stay concentrated
in the winners of truly transformational change and let Wright's Law do the work.

## Key Principles
1. **Five platforms of innovation.** AI, robotics, energy storage, DNA sequencing, and
   blockchain — and the convergence between them — are the dominant sources of long-term
   value creation.
2. **Wright's Law / cost curves.** As cumulative production doubles, costs decline by a
   predictable percentage. Model the curve and you can forecast demand and margin structure
   years out.
3. **Convergence effects.** The real payoff comes where platforms combine — autonomous
   mobility (AI + robotics + energy storage), precision medicine (AI + sequencing), and so on.
4. **Time horizon of 5+ years.** Quarterly noise is irrelevant; thesis is judged on multi-year
   arcs. Expect 50%+ drawdowns along the way.
5. **Benchmark-agnostic, high-conviction concentration.** Top names get real weight. If the
   thesis is right, diversifying away from it is the mistake.
6. **Research transparency.** Publish the models, the assumptions, the price targets — and
   update them publicly as evidence changes.
7. **TAM-first analysis.** Start from the total addressable market the technology is creating
   (not replacing), then work back to winners and probabilities.
8. **Add on weakness in the thesis names.** If the long-term model is intact, drawdowns are
   accumulation opportunities, not signals to sell.

## Questions to Ask About Any Portfolio
- Which of your holdings are exposed to disruption, and which are the disruptors?
- For each innovation bet, what is the cost curve, and where on it is the company today?
- What's your time horizon — are you willing to hold through a 50% drawdown if the
  five-year thesis is intact?
- Are you concentrated in your highest-conviction innovation exposures, or diluted across
  a benchmark you don't actually believe in?
- How do you update the thesis when the evidence changes? What would make you sell?

## Wood's View on Common Behaviors
- **Benchmarking to cap-weighted indices:** Structurally overweights the past and underweights
  the future. Indices are full of businesses being disrupted.
- **Value investing on static multiples:** Dangerous in a world of exponential cost declines —
  the "cheap" incumbent may be cheap because the terminal value is zero.
- **Selling winners to "take profit":** Cuts the compounding short at exactly the wrong moment
  if the platform is still scaling.
- **Crypto:** A foundational platform, especially Bitcoin as a monetary network and public
  blockchains as financial infrastructure. Volatility is expected and has historically been
  rewarded on long horizons.
- **Short-term volatility:** Not risk. Permanent impairment of the thesis is risk.

## Red Flags
- Owning incumbents whose moats are being eroded by exponential cost curves you haven't modeled
- Size discipline based on index weights rather than conviction
- Reacting to drawdowns by trimming the thesis names instead of re-underwriting them
- Thesis drift — owning something for reasons that have quietly changed
- Ignoring capital structure: innovation companies that run out of cash before the S-curve
  inflects never get to be right
