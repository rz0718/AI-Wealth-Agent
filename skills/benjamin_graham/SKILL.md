# Benjamin Graham — The Intelligent Investor's Framework

## Core Philosophy
Investment is most intelligent when it is most businesslike. An investor calculates what a
security is worth based on the facts; a speculator bets on what others will pay for it.
The market (Mr. Market) is a manic-depressive business partner — use his mood swings, don't
be governed by them. Your edge is temperament, not forecasting.

## Key Principles
1. **Investment vs. speculation.** An investment promises safety of principal and an adequate
   return after thorough analysis. Anything else is speculation — and must be sized accordingly.
2. **Margin of safety.** The single most important concept in investing. Always buy with a
   cushion between price and conservatively estimated intrinsic value.
3. **Mr. Market is your servant, not your guide.** Prices are quotes offered to you daily;
   you are never obligated to transact.
4. **Intrinsic value is anchored in the numbers.** Earnings power, asset value, and dividend
   record — not narratives, not growth fantasies.
5. **Defensive vs. enterprising investor.** Know which you are. The defensive investor seeks
   adequate returns with minimum effort; the enterprising investor earns more only through
   genuinely deeper work.
6. **Diversification within value.** Spread across 10–30 undervalued securities that each
   individually pass the tests — not as protection against ignorance, but against bad luck.
7. **Formula discipline beats forecasting.** Rule-based buying (e.g., net-nets, low P/E with
   strong balance sheets) outperforms confident predictions of the future.

## Questions to Ask About Any Portfolio
- For each holding, what is your estimate of intrinsic value, and what margin of safety does
  today's price give you?
- Are you invested, or are you speculating? Be honest about which bucket each position sits in.
- Would you still want to own this business if the stock market closed for five years?
- Is the balance sheet strong enough to survive a deep recession without forced dilution?
- Are you letting Mr. Market's mood dictate your actions, or your own valuation work?

## Graham's View on Common Behaviors
- **Crypto and non-cash-producing assets:** Outside the framework entirely — no earnings,
  no assets, no way to compute intrinsic value. Pure speculation on price.
- **Growth investing at any price:** Dangerous. Paying up for growth erodes margin of safety;
  most growth forecasts disappoint.
- **Market timing and momentum:** "The investor's chief problem — and even his worst enemy —
  is likely to be himself."
- **Leverage and margin:** Permanent loss of capital is the only real risk; leverage magnifies it.
- **Concentration without deep analysis:** Acceptable only for the truly enterprising investor
  with verified edge.

## Red Flags
- Paying prices that leave no margin of safety, justified by optimistic forecasts
- Owning a security you couldn't value with pencil and paper from the financial statements
- Weak balance sheets, aggressive accounting, or reliance on continued capital market access
- Confusing a good company with a good investment — price matters
- Buying because it's going up; selling because it's going down
