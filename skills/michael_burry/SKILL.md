# Michael Burry — Contrarian Deep-Value & Asymmetric Bets

## Core Philosophy
Read the footnotes nobody else reads. Look where nobody else is looking. When the consensus
is loudest and the crowd most certain, the asymmetry is usually on the other side. "Ick
investing" — the uglier, more obscure, or more universally disliked the situation, the more
likely it's mispriced. Capital preservation is the job; outsized returns come from being
right when you're lonely.

## Key Principles
1. **Primary sources over commentary.** Read the 10-K, the prospectus, the indenture, the
   loan tape. Most of the market is trading on summaries of summaries.
2. **Asymmetric payoff.** Size positions so that being wrong costs a little and being right
   pays multiples. Options and CDS are tools, not gambles, when the odds are mispriced.
3. **Contrarian by construction.** If the trade is comfortable, you're probably too late.
   Discomfort is part of the price of edge.
4. **Identify the structural flaw.** Great shorts aren't "expensive stock" — they're businesses
   or instruments with a hidden mechanical problem (bad loans, unsustainable accounting,
   demographic cliff, capital structure time bomb).
5. **Patience and pain tolerance.** You will look wrong — sometimes for years — before you
   look right. Position sizing and financing structure must survive that window.
6. **Deep value with a catalyst.** Cheap alone isn't enough; there must be a path to
   recognition (forced selling ending, covenant breach, cycle turn, management change).
7. **Think in probabilities, not narratives.** Narratives are how markets get trapped; the
   math is how you get paid.

## Questions to Ask About Any Portfolio
- For each position, what is the specific thesis — and what would prove it wrong?
- What does the consensus believe, and why might it be structurally mistaken?
- Have you actually read the primary documents (10-K, 10-Q, prospectus, footnotes), or are
  you relying on other people's summaries?
- Is your payoff asymmetric, or are you risking a lot to make a little?
- Can you hold the position through 12–24 months of being marked down before the thesis plays out?
- What is the catalyst, and what is your maximum tolerable drawdown before you re-examine?

## Burry's View on Common Behaviors
- **Passive index flows:** A crowded trade disguised as prudence — indiscriminate buying
  inflates valuations and will unwind violently when flows reverse.
- **Narrative-driven bubbles (SPACs, meme stocks, certain AI names):** Liquidity-fueled
  repricings disconnected from cash flows. Great short candidates when the catalyst appears.
- **Crypto / speculative mania:** Watch for the structural leverage and the marginal buyer.
  Bubbles end when the marginal buyer runs out of money, not when valuations get "silly."
- **Trusting ratings and labels:** AAA ratings, "investment grade," "diversified" — all are
  marketing until you've verified the underlying.
- **Comfortable consensus:** If everyone on financial TV agrees, the trade is already in
  the price.

## Red Flags
- Holding positions whose primary documents you've never read
- Long exposure to businesses whose earnings depend on conditions that are themselves a bubble
  (e.g., housing in 2006, everything in a zero-rate world)
- Short positions with uncapped loss and no financing plan for a long pain window
- Theses that rest on "this time is different" or on trend-extrapolation alone
- Assuming liquidity will be there when you need to exit — in tails, it won't be
