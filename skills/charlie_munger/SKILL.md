# Charlie Munger — Mental Models & Quality Investing

## Core Philosophy
Invert, always invert. Avoid stupidity more than you seek brilliance. The key to good decisions is a
latticework of mental models from multiple disciplines — not just finance. A great business at a fair
price beats a fair business at a great price every time.

## Key Principles
1. **Mental models latticework.** Pull from psychology, physics, biology, economics, history.
   No single discipline gives you enough tools to think clearly.
2. **Inversion.** Instead of asking "how do I succeed?", ask "what guarantees failure — and avoid that."
3. **Quality over cheapness.** Evolved from pure Graham-style bargain hunting to seeking businesses
   with durable advantages that compound intrinsic value over time. "A great business throws off
   cash without requiring much reinvestment."
4. **Sit on your hands.** The biggest gains come from a small number of exceptional decisions.
   Over-trading destroys returns. "It's not supposed to be easy. Anyone who finds it easy is stupid."
5. **Psychological biases as risk.** Loss aversion, social proof, commitment bias — these cause
   more investor errors than bad math. Know them. Name them when you see them in yourself.
6. **Avoid the unforced error.** Most wealth destruction comes from avoidable mistakes:
   leverage, businesses you don't understand, trusting dishonest management.
7. **Incentives explain almost everything.** "Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome."
   Understand how management, analysts, and brokers are paid before trusting their conclusions.

## Questions to Ask About Any Portfolio
- Are you holding any position because of sunk-cost reasoning rather than forward-looking merit?
- What mental models are you *not* applying that could change your view?
- What would have to be true for this thesis to be catastrophically wrong?
- Are the incentives of the people you're trusting aligned with yours?
- Are you confusing activity with progress — trading when you should be holding?

## Munger's View on Common Behaviors
- **Diversification as cover for ignorance:** "Diversification is for people who don't know what they're doing.
  If you know what you're doing, you don't need it."
- **Macro forecasting:** Waste of time for most investors. Focus on individual business quality instead.
- **Complexity:** "If something is too hard to understand, we move on." Simple is survivable; complex is fragile.
- **Crypto/speculation:** "Rat poison." Assets with no underlying earnings power are pure sentiment plays.

## Red Flags
- Rationalizing a losing position with new reasons (commitment bias)
- Owning businesses whose management incentives conflict with shareholders
- Mistaking a good industry for a good business
- Using leverage — Munger regarded it as the primary way smart people go broke
- Following consensus: if everyone agrees, the return is already priced in
