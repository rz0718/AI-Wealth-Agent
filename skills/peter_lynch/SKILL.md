# Peter Lynch — Invest in What You Know

## Core Philosophy
The average investor has real edges over Wall Street: you see products before analysts do,
you shop at the stores, you use the services. Turn that everyday observation into rigorous
research and you can beat the pros. Know what you own and know why you own it — in two
minutes or less.

## Key Principles
1. **Invest in what you know.** Use your professional expertise and consumer experience as
   idea sources — then do the homework to confirm the story.
2. **The two-minute drill.** If you can't explain in two minutes why you own a stock — what
   the business does, why it will grow, what could go wrong — you don't own it, it owns you.
3. **Categorize every stock.** Slow growers, stalwarts, fast growers, cyclicals, turnarounds,
   and asset plays each need different expectations and exit rules.
4. **PEG ratio.** A fairly-priced growth company's P/E should roughly equal its earnings
   growth rate. Below 1 is interesting; above 2 is expensive.
5. **Small is beautiful.** Fast growers in the 20–25% range, not yet discovered by Wall Street,
   are where ten-baggers live. Huge companies produce small moves.
6. **Love boring.** Dull names, ugly industries, spinoffs, and companies with silly tickers
   are often mispriced because nobody wants to talk about them at cocktail parties.
7. **Watch earnings and the balance sheet.** Growth must be real, funded by operations, with
   a clean balance sheet — not by debt or dilution.
8. **Hold your flowers, cut your weeds.** Average investors do the opposite — they sell
   winners to feel smart and hold losers hoping to get even.

## Questions to Ask About Any Portfolio
- Can you state the two-minute story for every position you own?
- What category is each stock (slow grower / stalwart / fast grower / cyclical / turnaround /
  asset play), and are you managing it accordingly?
- Where did the idea come from — genuine insight, or a tip / headline?
- Are you paying a PEG that makes sense for the growth you're actually getting?
- Are you watering the weeds and cutting the flowers?

## Lynch's View on Common Behaviors
- **Macro forecasting:** "If you spend 13 minutes a year on economics, you've wasted 10 minutes."
  Focus on companies, not predictions about rates or GDP.
- **Hot tips:** Treat them as starting points for research, never as buy signals.
- **Missing a move:** There will always be another ten-bagger. Patience beats chasing.
- **Over-diversification in a personal account:** Owning too many names you can't track is
  "de-worsification." 3–10 well-understood names beats 40 half-followed ones.
- **Selling winners too early:** The biggest gains come in years 3, 4, 5+ of a great business —
  not in the first 30% pop.

## Red Flags
- Owning a stock whose two-minute story you can't tell
- Applying fast-grower P/Es to cyclicals near peak earnings, or stalwart P/Es to fading businesses
- "Diworsification" — the company buying unrelated businesses with its cash
- Rising debt funding the growth story, rather than operations
- Falling in love with the story and ignoring deteriorating fundamentals
